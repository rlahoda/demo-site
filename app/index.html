Conversatio

October 2019, Issue 1

What is Spiritual Formation?

You may be familiar with the term discipleship but maybe not spiritual formation. In discipleship, our participation with God in His story makes us disciples. This is our status. Spiritual formation is the inner process of discipleship. With our participation, Christ is at work internally forming us through His Spirit so that our character can reflect His image.

We were created in God's image to reflect His image!

26a"Then God said, "Let us make human beings in our image, to be like us. … 27So God created human beings in his own image. In the image of God he created them; male and female he created them."
(Genesis 1:26a-27, NLT)

Then God blessed Adam and Eve and gave them three assignments (Gen 1:28):

  1. Be fruitful and multiply
  2. Fill and subdue the earth,
  3. govern/reign

Let's look at these assignments in light of humanity as image bearers:

  1. Be fruitful and multiply His image
  2. to fill Earth with His image
  3. in order to govern and reign to reflect His image over all creation.

Then sin beget sin, and you know the story.

We know that in Christ, we are new creations, the old has gone and the new is here (2 Cor 5:17). But sometimes, or maybe even often, we don't feel like new creations do we? We get stuck in old sin patterns. We respond too harshly, we lack patience, we rush and don't see someone in need. This is why Paul tells us to "continue to work out your salvation with fear and trembling…" (Philippians 2:12b-13, NIV) Notice that Paul beckons us to action despite God working in us. This is good news! We are to be neither puppets nor puppeteers but partners with God, colaboring with Him in writing our shared story.

What does this have to do with Spiritual Formation?

Our spirits are constantly being formed whether or not we are Christian. We have the same assignment today that God gave Adam and Eve! We need to colabor with God so that our reflection mirrors Christ's image to the world. This involves being rooted in spiritual formation practices. Just as a tree's roots not only anchor it in the rich soil and supply it with water and nutrients but also keep the surrounding soil from being washed away, likewise, spiritual formation anchors us relationally in Christ, quenching our thirst and allowing us to absorb His richness so that we can be an anchor for others.

1"Since, then, you have been raised with Christ, set your hearts on things above, where Christ is, seated at the right hand of God. 2Set your minds on things above, not on earthly things."
(Colossians 3:1-2 NIV)

A foundational practice in spiritual formation is setting our hearts and minds on things above. Dallas Willard says that God is as close as the air we breathe. God is not sitting up somewhere distant and uninvolved, so think of above not as the physical distance to God but as the distance from ordinary to holy. When we focus our minds and hearts on God, we become self-forgetting. We can then align our will with His.

The life of faith is not done to us
but developed in us.

Eugene Peterson

Spiritual Practices

Lectio Divina: Holy Reading

  • Read Colossians 3:1-17
  • Memorize or Pray the passage
  • Practice Meditative Prayer. This kind of prayer involves the mind and the heart (as Col 3:1-2 mentions). The Bible is God’s living word. It was written to people in a specific time and place, but it is for us today as well. When we pray scripture meditatively, we ask God to show us what the passage means to us. My favorite meditative prayer is lectio divina (pronounced lexio diveena).

Ready/Preparation

  • Relax and come fully into the present
  • Become consciously aware of God's presence with you
  • Express your willingness to hear from God with a brief prayer such as "Come Lord Jesus" or "Here I am."

Read (Lectio, Reading)

  • Read slowly, pausing between phrases and sentences, and let your awareness rest on each word. This text is for all who are thirsty.
  • As you reread, listen for the word or phrase that strikes you or catches your attention. This word is meant for you.

Reflect (Meditatio, Meditation)

  • Read the passage again and listen for the way this word connects to your life
  • This is the Living Word of God. How is my present life touched by this word?
  • What is it in my life right now that needs to hear this word?

Respond (Oratio, Prayer)

  • Read the passage again.
  • What is God trying to say to me?
  • What is my response to God based on what I have read and encountered?
  • Enter into a dialogue with God; listen for your deepest and truest response and allow your prayer to flow.
  • Share with God any feeling the text has aroused: joy, fear, sorrow, anger, love, conviction, desire, need, comfort, peace
  • Is God calling you to act or respond? It may be helpful to journal.

Rest (Contemplatio, Contemplation)

  • Read the passage one last time.
  • Release and return to a quiet, wordless rest in God and His Word.
  • Rest in His one-of-a-kind, unconditional love.

Resolve/Return

  • Resolve to carry this word with you and live it out in the context of daily life.
  • As you listen for this word throughout your day, you will be led deeper into its meaning, until it begins to live in you and you enflesh this word.
    It may be helpful for some to turn their word or phrase into a short prayer or choose an image, picture, or symbol that you can carry to remind you of it.
  • Explore the question, Who is God?

About the Newsletter

Richard J. Foster, the founder of Renovaré, says Conversatio morum is hard to translate. In Prayer: Finding the Heart’s True Home, Foster says, “Negatively, it means death to the status quo, death to things as they have always been. Positively, it means constant change, constant conversion, constant openness to the movings of the Spirit.”

I wanted to name this newsletter something that would highlight the very nature of spiritual formation, that is, that it is a transformational process. Chris Hall, the president of Renovaré says, “Spiritual formation is the slowest of all Christian movements.” Take heart though, patience is a fruit of the Spirit. As we progress in the Way, we become more fruitful.

Spiritual formation practices are like any practice we pursue be it athletics, the arts, or others. We must train to progress. This involves growth and change. As we progress on our faith journey, we become more and more like Christ. We transform. My hope is to combine and share my vocations of writing and teaching spiritual formation so that we can grow together in Christ to reflect His image to the world. So be it.
